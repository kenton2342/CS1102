Proof-of-Work (PoW)



One of the earliest and most widely used models is Proof-of-Work (PoW). For example, in Bitcoin PoW systems, miners compete to solve complex cryptographic puzzles, typically using the SHA-256 algorithm, to validate transactions and add new blocks to the chain.



The first miner to solve the puzzle would receive a reward in cryptocurrency. This mechanism provides a high security level due to the difficulty, as altering the blockchain would require immense computational power, making it resistant to attacks (Kiayias, 2021). However, PoW is highly energy-intensive, with Bitcoin alone consuming an estimated 150 terawatt-hours (TWh) of electricity annually (Uni of Cambridge, 2025).



Additionally, the increasing dominance of large mining pools introduces centralization risks, potentially undermining the decentralized purpose of blockchain systems.
